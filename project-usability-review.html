The Brief

Not every project starts with a design brief.

This one started with a conversation. A client based in Australia, running an indoor sports and trampoline centre, was days away from launching a new website. Before going live, they wanted a professional set of eyes on it.

Not to redesign it. Not to rebuild it from scratch. Just to catch what they might have missed, and make sure users would actually be able to find what they were looking for — and convert.

DZiel Creative was brought in to conduct a full usability evaluation across the entire site, covering five key pages: Home, Party, Contact Us, About Us, and Gallery.

Methodology

What Is a Usability Evaluation?

A usability evaluation assesses how easy and enjoyable it is for users to achieve their goals while using a product. It can be carried out at any stage of design or development, and multiple methods are available depending on the project goals, timeline, and resources.

For this project, an expert review method was used — an experienced UX professional assessing the site against usability goals, accessibility standards, design principles, and heuristics. Unlike usability testing, it doesn't involve live users, which makes it particularly well-suited for pre-launch reviews where the goal is to catch issues before real users ever encounter them.

The goal isn't to criticise. The goal is to identify anything that could confuse users, prevent conversions, or create a poor experience — and then provide clear, actionable recommendations to fix it.

This evaluation went beyond pure usability. Foundational SEO and CRO knowledge was applied throughout, and coding knowledge was used to inspect the underlying code — identifying technical errors and flagging areas where development best practices hadn't been followed.

Approach

Conducted Without AI Tools — And Why That Mattered

This evaluation was completed entirely through human expert review. No AI tools were used to analyse user behaviour, and there were three clear reasons for that.

First, the site wasn't live yet. The development link wasn't publicly accessible, which meant most AI scanning tools couldn't reach it. Second, when AI has been used in past evaluations — feeding screenshots and asking for analysis — the results needed double-checking for inconsistencies. That back-and-forth actually took more time than doing the analysis directly.

Third, this evaluation required going beyond surface-level observations: testing forms, checking interactions, tracing user flows step by step — all with a focus on lead generation. That level of depth isn't something current AI tools can reliably replicate.

The goal has always been to deliver documentation that goes deep, focusing on CRO and real usability impact — not just a list of observations that looks good on paper.

The Challenge

Writing for Two Very Different Audiences

One of the biggest challenges of this project had nothing to do with the website itself. It was the evaluation document.

The client had no UX background. The development team, on the other hand, needed technical clarity. Writing one document that could genuinely serve both audiences — without dumbing things down for the developers or overwhelming the client with jargon — required real intentionality.

Every finding was written with plain language first, followed by a clear rationale. Not "this violates Nielsen's heuristic of consistency and standards" — but "this is confusing because users expect the navigation and the footer to match, and right now they don't."

The client should be able to read it on their own and understand exactly what was wrong and why it mattered. The developers should be able to pick it up and know exactly what to fix.

What Was Uncovered

Rather than listing every single finding, the focus below is on the ones that tell the most interesting story.

01
The icon that looked like a gun
The "Why Choose Us" section featured an infographic — a great idea for communicating value quickly. But the icon chosen to represent "Supervised Fun" looked, unmistakably, like a gun. On a family-focused indoor sports website aimed at children's birthday parties, this was exactly the kind of detail that needed catching before launch. The section also had a busy background image placed behind the infographic text, making the content almost impossible to read.
Visual Design · CRO
02
The party page that couldn't book a party
On the Trampoline Party page, the main CTA directed users to "Book a Jump Pass" — on a page specifically about booking a party. Users who had already decided they wanted a party were being sent somewhere else entirely. The other two party pages on the site had a booking form built right in. The Trampoline Party page had none. Users who made it to that page had no direct way to book — an obvious and easily fixable conversion gap.
CRO · User Flows · Missing Element
03
Red used as a warning — on a party page
Red is psychologically associated with danger, warnings, and stop signals. On the party page, red was used to highlight a note reminding users to bring their own cake. The intention was helpful but the experience felt like a warning. Red also made up approximately 60% of the visual weight across the site — good visual design follows the 60–30–10 rule, but the dominant colour should support the experience. Here, it was working against it.
Colour Theory · Visual Design
04
The CTA that disappeared on mobile
The homepage had a clear "Contact Us" CTA on desktop. On mobile, it was gone entirely. The majority of users browse on their phones — a missing CTA on mobile is a missing conversion opportunity. Several other elements were also broken or misaligned on mobile, suggesting the mobile experience hadn't been thoroughly reviewed before launch.
Responsive Design · Mobile UX · CRO
05
SEO issues that would have hurt from day one
Not a single page on the site had an H1 tag — every page needs exactly one. Without it, the site was essentially invisible to Google from a structural standpoint. Multiple images were also missing ALT tags, affecting both SEO and accessibility. And a data collection form was live without a privacy policy notice — a legal requirement when collecting personal information like email addresses and phone numbers.
SEO · Accessibility · Legal Compliance
06
The small things that add up
Beyond the headline issues: hover effects on non-clickable elements that confused users, external links opening in the same tab, navigation showing two party options in one place and three in another, a contact form that allowed submission without a message, and a gallery carousel on mobile that worked once and then stopped responding. Individually minor — but together they create an experience that feels unpolished and erodes user trust.
Interaction Design · Trust · Consistency
The Outcome

The Client's Response

After completing the evaluation, DZiel Creative presented the findings to the client. The document was structured to walk through each page clearly, with every issue explained in plain language, supported by visual callouts and screenshots, and paired with a specific recommendation.

"Just read the whole PDF audit you have done, love the attention to detail!! I will definitely have to get you on a call with the guys and lets go through this together. There's one more website coming too to audit, will send that after we finalise this one. Thanks for all the work."

— Mr. V., Company Director

That message said a lot. Not just that the client was happy — but that they trusted the process enough to want to bring the development team in, and that they were already thinking about the next project.

What Happened Next

The development team reviewed the evaluation and began implementing the recommended changes. A follow-up review was conducted once the updates were made — and while many issues had been addressed, there were still significant items outstanding. This is normal. A usability evaluation isn't a one-and-done exercise. Real improvement is iterative.

The site is still in the pre-launch phase, with final fixes being worked through before the go-live date. DZiel Creative remains involved, and a meeting with the development team is scheduled to walk through the remaining items together. A second website project has already been confirmed.

Planning for Real User Data

One recommendation went beyond the evaluation itself. Once the site goes live, the real learning begins. Even the most thorough expert evaluation is based on expert judgement — it can't replace data from real users actually using the site. The client was advised to implement heatmaps and behaviour tracking tools post-launch, to capture patterns that no pre-launch review can predict.
